Wind Tunnel

December 2025 - April 2026

What started as a way to pass time turned into a full-scale design and fabrication project. I initially modeled a wind tunnel in Fusion 360 for fun, but after refining the CAD, I became determined to bring it to life.

Airflow Optimization: Using a 521 CFM fan, I designed a honeycomb flow straightener to reduce turbulence and eliminate eddy currents, producing a more uniform laminar flow. I also reduced the cross-sectional area of the test section relative to the fan diameter to increase air velocity.

Fabrication: The wind tunnel body is fully 3D printed and assembled using heat-set M3 threaded inserts and machine screws. The outer frame was CNC-machined from plywood and stained to enhance both structural integrity and aesthetics.

Electromagnet: To counteract drag forces from high-speed airflow, I designed an electromagnet by winding enameled copper wire around an iron core, mounted beneath the test section to stabilize test objects during operation.

Three-Laned Flywheel Projectile Launcher

Janurary 2026 - April 2026

While competing in the FIRST Robotics Competition with Team 2551, I led the development of our robot’s primary scoring mechanism: a projectile launcher designed to consistently shoot game pieces into a target.

Flywheel: I developed a three-lane flywheel system to maximize scoring throughput. By increasing the flywheel’s moment of inertia, we minimized velocity drops during rapid firing, resulting in significantly improved shot consistency.

Adjustable Hood: To account for high-speed movement and variable shooting distances, I designed an adjustable hood mechanism that allowed us to dynamically control launch angle. This gave the robot flexibility to score from multiple positions on the field.

In addition to system design, I mentored underclassmen in Onshape and introduced them to advanced fabrication methods including CNC machining, 3D printing, and rapid prototyping.

Through iterative design and team collaboration, we overcame multiple challenges and placed 8th out of 45 teams at the San Francisco District Qualifying Competition.

Floating-Arm Trebuchet

March 2025 - May 2025

Tasked with converting gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy, my team chose to build a floating-arm trebuchet due to its high efficiency and reduced energy loss.

Arm Optimization: I CNC-machined weight-reduction holes along the arm to lower its moment of inertia, increasing angular acceleration. We also used medium-soft skateboard wheels to improve energy transfer efficiency.

Deployable Trusses: To handle unexpected structural stresses in multiple directions, we designed adjustable trusses that could be repositioned for stability on uneven surfaces.

The project followed a full engineering workflow: initial sketches, prototyping with mock materials, CAD modeling in Fusion 360, G-code generation, CNC fabrication, and final assembly.

120+ Lbs Climbing Mechanism

Janurary 2025 - April 2025

In my first year competing in the FIRST Robotics Competition, I led the design and development of our robot’s climbing mechanism.

Spring-Loaded Hooks: I implemented torsion spring-loaded hooks that maintained outward force, ensuring reliable engagement with the climbing bar during operation.

Lightweight Arm: To optimize performance, I machined weight-reduction contours into the arm while maintaining structural integrity. I also calculated the required gear ratios to lift over 120 lbs and designed a custom gearbox and pulley system to achieve this.

May 2025 - Present

After experiencing the impact of being part of a robotics team, I was inspired to create that same environment at my own school. I founded the Chilla-Gorillas Robotics Team at San Rafael High School to build both technical skills and community.

Rocket Fuel Powered Dragster

December 2024 - April 2025

I designed and built a wooden dragster powered by a solid-fuel rocket engine, competing in a timed race format.

Design Iteration: After observing poor performance in the initial run, I applied Newton’s Second Law (F = ma) and focused on reducing mass to improve acceleration and top speed.

Results: While the original design performed poorly, iterative improvements led to a final version that achieved the fastest time in the competition.

Arched Wooden Suspension Bridge

September 2024 - November 2024

For a structural design competition, I studied real-world bridge engineering and drew inspiration from the Sydney Harbour Bridge.

Arch Modeling: I used Desmos to model hyperbolic functions that approximate load distribution under gravity, then applied trigonometry to determine precise cutting angles for each structural member.

This project combined mathematical modeling with hands-on fabrication to create an efficient and visually compelling structure.
